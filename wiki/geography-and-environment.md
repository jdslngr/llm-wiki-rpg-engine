# Geography & Environment

## Local Geography

The Archipelago Lighthouse, where Kaspen lives, is located on the island of **Panay** in the Philippines, specifically on the southern side of the island. Panay itself sits in the central and western region of the archipelago. During this period, sea levels were about 10 meters lower than today, making most islands considerably larger. To the east, just a couple of islands away from Panay, lies what is now known as the Pacific Ocean.

At this time, the island of Negros and Panay were much closer together, connected by land bridges. The **Kanlaon volcano** stood out as a prominent feature on the southeastern horizon when viewed from the Archipelago Lighthouse.

The island of Panay is characterized by large open woodlands and grasslands, with dense and deep forests concentrated on its eastern side. A mountain range runs north to south along the western portion of the island. Near these mountains, vegetation flourishes, nourished by river runoff. As one moves toward the distant sea, the landscape becomes saltier and drier, giving way to hardy scrub, coastal grasses, and mangroves.

Massive, fast-moving rivers shape canyons through the mountains before spilling out onto the plains. These rivers serve as the lifeblood for both animals and early humans — Kaspen could easily spend all morning watching the activity along their banks.

The high peaks of these mountains are often shrouded in mossy, damp, and cool vegetation. On the northeast side of Panay, striking karst formations appear as massive, white rocky hills with jagged tops and deep caves, rising from a wide, grassy coastal plain.

The shoreline on the south side of Panay, where the lighthouse stands, is a coastal plain made up of hardened silt, river deposits, and ancient coral limestone exposed to the air. This area stretches into an expanse of tall tropical grasses, punctuated by clusters of fire-resistant trees.

## Local Climate

There are two seasons: dry and wet.

**During the dry season**, the mountain range plays a crucial role in shaping the climate. Acting as a massive wall, it creates a rain shadow effect: while the eastern side of the island (the plains) occasionally receives showers, the western shelf remains sheltered and dry. With sea levels lower than today, much more landmass is exposed across Southeast Asia, resulting in air that sweeps over Panay being continentally dry. Visually, this transforms the wide 10km coastal shelf into a brittle, toasted brown landscape. Dust from the dry plains of "Greater Negros-Panay" often hazes the horizon, making Mount Kanlaon appear ghostlike in the distance.

**When the wet season arrives**, at the onset of the monsoon season, Western Panay bears the brunt of winds coming off the Sulu Sea. Although storms may be less frequent than today due to slightly cooler ocean surfaces 100,000 years ago, the ones that do occur are massive. The first rains trigger megafloods as rugged mountains and flat coastal shelves combine to funnel torrents of water across the land. Rivers, which appear as silver threads during the dry season, swell into miles-wide brown floods, carrying uprooted trees and sediment out to sea. Within just a few days of the first rainfall, the previously dead brown coastal shelf explodes into a violent, electric green.

These climate patterns have profound implications for the story's setting. The average temperature is about 2°C cooler than today, and on clear January nights during the dry season, the Panay shelf can drop to 18°C or 19°C — cold enough to make early humans accustomed to tropical warmth shiver. The monsoon thunderstorms over the sea, unobstructed by modern light pollution, provide a spectacle. Kaspen and Kaelen can sit atop limestone cliffs and watch lightning illuminate the entire 10km shelf, revealing the silhouettes of huddled stegodons or hunters seeking refuge in caves.

## Significant Natural Features

### Geographic & Geological Features

- **Central Panay Mountain Range:** A prominent, jagged spine made up of volcanic and metamorphic rock. This range acts as a significant wall, shielding the western coast and causing it to be much drier than the eastern side during the Amihan (northeast) season.
- **Western Coastal Shelf:** A broad, flat expanse of exposed seabed, stretching 5–10 km wide. This newly uncovered territory consists of savannah and scrubland, lying between the mountain foothills and the edge of the Sulu Sea.
- **"V" Canyons:** Deep, steep-sided valleys created by rivers (such as the ancient Sibalom and Paliwan), providing the main natural passages for humans and animals to descend from the high mountain jungles to the coastal plains.
- **Limestone Karst Outcrops:** Towering, white-grey rock formations of ancient coral limestone, especially prominent in the northeast (Gigantes) and scattered along the northern coast. These outcrops are riddled with cool, deep caves.

### Vegetation & Climate Profile

- **Highland Cloud Forests:** Permanently damp, moss-covered forests on the mountain peaks, dominated by tropical oaks.
- **The Savannah Mosaic:** The dominant landscape of the lowlands. Vast grasslands (Sorghum and Cogongrass) dotted with fire-resistant trees like Acacia and Albizia.
- **Riparian Corridors:** "Galleries" of dense, evergreen jungle that grew only along the banks of the rivers, snaking through the otherwise dry plains.

### Fauna (Animals)

- **_Stegodon luzonensis_:** Small, island-dwelling relatives of elephants with massive, straight tusks.
- **Cagayan Giant Pig:** A large-tusked, powerful pig species that roamed the savannah corridors.
- **Philippine Rhinoceros:** A species of rhino adapted to the open woodlands of the archipelago.
- **Giant Monitor Lizards:** Massive, dragon-like reptiles that were the primary land predators of the plains.
- **Endemic Deer & Pigs:** Ancestors of the Visayan Spotted Deer and Warty Pig, moving in much larger herds than today's survivors.

### Land Bridges & Nearby Islands

100,000 years ago, "Panay" was just one part of a larger landmass.

- **The Guimaras Land Bridge:** Guimaras was not an island; it was a hilly region of central Panay. You could walk from what is now Iloilo City to Guimaras without getting your feet wet.
- **The Negros Connection:** The Panay Gulf was much smaller. A wide land bridge connected Southeastern Panay directly to Negros. This allowed animals to migrate freely between the two regions.
- **The "Visible" Kanlaon:** Because of this connection, the massive Mount Kanlaon on Negros was a landmark on the eastern horizon, often venting steam.
- **The Mindoro Gap:** To the north, the sea was narrower but likely still present. Palawan and Mindoro were much closer, acting as "stepping stones" for species (and perhaps the early *Homo luzonensis*) arriving from mainland Asia.

## Continents & Landmasses

The landmasses are largely similar to current day in Southeast Asia where the story mainly takes place.

**Africa** is the center of the human world 100,000 years ago. The Sahara is not the vast, hyper-arid desert we know today; instead, it cycles through "Green Sahara" phases, forming a mosaic of lakes, rivers, and grasslands that allow *Homo sapiens* to move freely across the continent. The Congo Basin is a dense rainforest, serving as a vital sanctuary for wildlife.

**Eurasia** is a massive, continuous stretch of land dominated by megafauna such as mammoths and woolly rhinoceroses. In Europe, the northern regions — including Scandinavia and the northern United Kingdom — are often under the Fennoscandian Ice Sheet, while the rest of the continent is a "Mammoth Steppe," a cold, dry yet productive grassland. Neanderthals reach their peak territory in the west, while Denisovans occupy the east. West Asia operates as a crossroads where *Homo sapiens* leaving Africa first encounter Neanderthals.

**Sahul**, or "Super-Australia," is a single massive continent that combines Australia, New Guinea, and Tasmania. The Wallace Line, a deep-water trench, separates Southeast Asia from Sahul, preventing most humans and animals from crossing by foot, so eventual crossings require boats.

**Sundaland**, or "Greater Southeast Asia," is much larger than current day Southeast Asia in terms of land area. The Sunda Shelf connects the islands of Borneo, Java, and Sumatra with the Malay Peninsula and parts of Vietnam and Thailand, creating a massive, fertile peninsula roughly the size of Europe. The Philippines is a separate archipelago that is divided by deep waters, but the smaller gaps make it easier for species to "island hop" from Sundaland into Panay or Luzon.

In the **Americas**, North and South America exist as isolated continents largely untouched by humans. These continents function as "waiting rooms," ruled entirely by megafauna such as giant ground sloths, sabertooth cats, and American camels. The Bering Land Bridge between Siberia and Alaska occasionally opens and closes as sea levels fluctuate, but humans do not yet cross it for many thousands of years.

**Antarctica** remains much as it is today: a vast, uninhabitable ice shield. However, its ice volume is significantly larger at this time, contributing to the lower global sea levels that shape the connected landmasses of Sundaland and Sahul.
